Thomas Lehmann

Edmonton, AB · Canada · (780) 604-2794 · thomasl86@gmail.com

I am an experienced robotics and mechatronics engineer with a wide range of experience in medical and industrial engineering research projects. My current work focuses on large-scale robotic additive manufacturing (AM) utilizing 6-axis robotic articulated arms and 2-axis workpiece positioning systems. Such systems are leveraged to facilitate support-free and large-scale printing of metal and polymer parts. Through this work, I have helped advance state of the art research into the industry through collaborations with InnoTech Alberta. I am passionate about robotic AM, which I believe to be a very exciting and rewarding field with potential to significantly transform, optimize and re-define existing industrial manufacturing technologies in order to satisfy the fast-changing product and technology demands of a highly connected 21st century.

I have been working with and conducting research on industrial robotics, mechatronics and automation research as a postdoctoral fellow in Dr. Ahmed Qureshi’s Additive Design and Manufacturing Systems (ADaMS) Laboratory at the University of Alberta in Edmonton, Alberta since October 2018. My research is mainly focused on large-scale robotic additive manufacturing with metal and polymer deposition, including developing a hardware-agnostic software toolchain for the execution of AM 3D printing trajectories with varying robotic and deposition systems, including in-situ process monitoring and sensing.

Through my work with the ADaMS lab, I have collaborated with InnoTech Alberta, where I worked closely with Dr Tonya Wolfe on research on parameter optimization during robotic WAAM. These research findings were published in the journal Materials & Design.

I supervise senior undergraduate and graduate research and development on a range of topics, including a laser-hybrid repair project and various designs of control solutions for custom 3-axis and 5-axis CNC-based AM and hybrid manufacturing platforms. Supervising and advising numerous undergraduate and graduate students on their research and development thorughout my academic career has given me important and valuable experience in leadership and management.

I received my PhD from the University of Alberta in 2018 under the supervision of Dr. Mahdi Tavakoli of the Telerobotic and Biorobotic Systems (TBS) Laboratory. During my doctoral research, I developed novel technologies for robotic assistance during the minimally invasive medical procedure prostate brachytherapy, including publishing original research on the estimation of needle deflection, trajectory prediction, and a novel method for the steering of flexible needles.

ROS-based Software Toolchain for Robotic Additive Manufacturing

Project Dates: October 2018 - Present

When I first joined the ADaMS lab at the University of Alberta, I began to explore how to execute 3D printing trajectories on industrial manipulators using ROS. With my prior experience with ROS-Industrial-based robot drivers, the kinematics framework MoveIt! and hardware integration via ROS, I was confident that it was possible to develop a software for execution of 3D printing trajectories using software frameworks I was already familiar with from past projects. This initial idea developed into a functional and hardware-agnostic AM software toolchain for the execution of planar and multi-axis AM trajectories with successful builds of demo components. While the toolchain has only been used on different Motoman robot models, initial tests in simulation show that it is possible to execute the same trajectories on ABB robots with no modification to the toolchain. One of the most attractive features of the toolchain is the hardware-agnostic and modular design that enables the quick integration of different deposition and robotic systems and extension with algorithms for the real-time monitoring and control of the deposition process. Below are some of the highlights, demo videos and pictures of builds completed with the AM toolchain. A special tank you goes to the students who helped with integration and development of software drivers for deposition systems. For robotics-centred data structure definitions and trajectory visualization, the toolchain uses a modified version of the ROS Additive Manufacturing stack.

  • Ability to take trajectories generated via Mastercam-Aplus (planar and multi-axis builds) and Cura (planar builds, re-configured to work for WAAM)
    • Ability to interpret RepRap (planar) and Mastercam-Aplus (planar and multi-axis) gcodes
  • Ability to incorporate sensing systems for sensor fusion, machine learning and control feedback
  • Where other softwares provide only open-loop trajectory execution (Robotmaster, Octopuz), our software has the potential ability to execute builds with closed-loop real-time process control
